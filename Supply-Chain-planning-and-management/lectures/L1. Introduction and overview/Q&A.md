## **1. A typical supply chain has a feed-forward flow of material and a feedback flow of information. Explain why this is the case.**

A typical supply chain has a feed-forward flow of material because physical products move downstream from suppliers through manufacturing and distribution stages to the final customer. At each stage value is added to the product, such as processing, assembly, packaging, or transportation. This forward movement reflects the physical transformation and delivery of goods.

In contrast, information flows in the opposite direction, from customers back upstream to manufacturers and suppliers. This feedback flow of information includes customer orders, sales data, forecasts, inventory levels, and demand signals. This information is essential for planning production, replenishment, and distribution activities. Without accurate and timely information flowing upstream, firms would be unable to plan effectively, leading to excess inventory, stockouts, or poor customer service. Therefore, supply chains require both material and information flows to operate efficiently and responsively.

## **2. Some supply chains are very complex involving many companies, others are simpler. Give examples of each and explain what the planning challenges might be in each case.**

Some supply chains are highly complex and involve many companies across multiple countries. An example is the aerospace or electronics industry, where products such as aircraft or smartphones rely on thousands of components sourced from a global network of suppliers. Planning challenges in such complex supply chains include long lead times, coordination across many independent firms, high supply risk, geopolitical uncertainty, and difficulties in demand forecasting and information sharing.

In contrast, simpler supply chains involve fewer stages and fewer firms. An example is a local dairy supply chain, where milk is collected from farms, processed, and delivered to nearby retailers. While planning is simpler, challenges still exist, such as managing perishable inventory, matching supply with fluctuating demand, and ensuring timely distribution. In both cases, effective planning is essential, but the scale and nature of the challenges differ significantly.

## **3. Some supply chains are mature and slow to change whilst others are new and still evolving. Give some examples of each.**

Mature supply chains are typically well established, with stable technologies, predictable demand patterns, and standardised processes. An example is the steel industry, which has existed for many decades and follows well-understood production and distribution processes. These supply chains tend to change slowly because investments are large, technologies are proven, and demand patterns are relatively stable.

In contrast, new and evolving supply chains are often associated with emerging technologies or new products. An example is the flash memory or electric vehicle supply chain, which has developed rapidly in recent years. These supply chains face challenges such as uncertain demand, rapid technological change, limited supplier capability, and frequent reconfiguration. Planning in such supply chains is more difficult because firms must adapt quickly to change and uncertainty.

## **5. Provide a definition for supply chain management and give some reasons why it is often very challenging for companies to manage their supply chain successfully.**

Supply chain management can be defined as the coordination and integration of suppliers, manufacturers, warehouses, distributors, and retailers to ensure that products and services are delivered to customers in the right quantities, at the right locations, at the right time, while minimising total system-wide costs and meeting service level requirements.

Managing the supply chain is challenging because it involves multiple independent organisations with different objectives and incentives. Demand is often uncertain and difficult to forecast accurately, while supply disruptions, capacity constraints, and transportation issues can further complicate planning. In addition, global supply chains are exposed to risks such as geopolitical instability, regulatory changes, and environmental concerns. These factors make coordination, information sharing, and decision-making across the supply chain complex and difficult.

## **6. What is a flow shop? Why are flow shops often preferred in production operations?**

A flow shop is a type of production system in which all products follow the same sequence of processing steps through the operation. Each product moves from one process to the next in a fixed order, often along a production line. Flow shops are commonly used in high-volume, standardised production environments.

Flow shops are often preferred because they are easier to plan, manage, and control than other types of production systems. The predictable routing allows for efficient scheduling, lower work-in-process inventory, and higher utilisation of resources. Flow shops also tend to achieve lower unit costs due to economies of scale and reduced variability, making them suitable for mass production of standard products.

## **7. What is the Customer Order Decoupling Point (CODP) and how does its location change in different kinds of production systems and supply chains?**

The Customer Order Decoupling Point (CODP) is the point in the supply chain where a specific customer order enters the operational system. Upstream of the CODP, activities are typically forecast-driven and planned in advance, while downstream of the CODP activities are triggered by actual customer orders.

The location of the CODP varies depending on the production system. In a Make-to-Stock (MTS) system, the CODP is located at the finished goods inventory, meaning customer orders are fulfilled from stock. In an Assemble-to-Order (ATO) system, the CODP is at the assembly stage. In a Make-to-Order (MTO) system, the CODP moves further upstream to the start of production, while in an Engineer-to-Order (ETO) system it is located at the design stage. The position of the CODP has important implications for inventory levels, lead times, responsiveness, and supply chain planning complexity.

## 8. Difference Between Make-to-Order (MTO) and Make-to-Stock (MTS) Systems

#### Differences

The primary difference between **Make-to-Order (MTO)** and **Make-to-Stock (MTS)** lies in the point in the operational system at which a customer order is fulfilled.

- In an **MTS system**, customer orders are satisfied from **finished goods inventory**.
- In an **MTO system**, **production does not begin until a customer order is received**.

Although this distinction appears simple, it has significant implications for forecasting, inventory management, lead times, and customer service.

---

#### Advantages and Disadvantages

**Make-to-Stock (MTS)**

- **Advantages**:
  - Enables rapid response to customer demand since products are already manufactured.
- **Disadvantages**:
  - Relies heavily on accurate demand forecasting.
  - Risk of excess or obsolete inventory if forecasts are incorrect.

**Make-to-Order (MTO)**

- **Advantages**:
  - Avoids holding finished goods inventory that may not sell.
  - Reduces the risk of obsolescence.
- **Disadvantages**:
  - Customers must wait for the product to be manufactured.
  - Longer lead times compared to MTS systems.

MTO systems typically hold inventory only in the form of raw materials.

---

#### When Is Each Approach Used?

- **Make-to-Stock (MTS)** is commonly used for **fast-moving, high-volume consumer products**, such as grocery food items or simple, low-value products. These products have predictable demand and a lower risk of not selling.

- **Make-to-Order (MTO)** is usually applied to **slower-moving, complex, specialised products** with **lower volumes and higher value**, such as industrial machinery. Customers are often willing to wait for these products to be produced.

---

#### Other Related Approaches

- **Assemble-to-Order (ATO)**: A hybrid approach between MTS and MTO, where components are stocked but final assembly occurs after an order is received.
- **Engineer-to-Order (ETO)**: The customer is involved in specifying or designing the product, which is then manufactured to order.

Many companies adopt a **combination of these approaches** depending on the nature of their products.
